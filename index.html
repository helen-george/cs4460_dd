Hurricane Risk and Response in the Southeastern U.S.

Jeff Yang, Libby Nesbitt, Helen George, Joanna Rose Zolnik

The Problem

Hurricanes pose a significant threat to communities across the United States, especially those in the Southeast â€” which is highly vulnerable due to its coastal geography, frequent intense storms, and limited resources in many communities for effective mitigation and infrastructure upgrades.

Hurricane risks are exacerbated by inadequate preparedness, outdated hazard maps, and variability in local governance and resource allocation. The National Flood Insurance Program (NFIP) and disaster mitigation efforts offer crucial mechanisms for mitigating these risks by enabling property owners to purchase insurance and incentivizing communities to adopt resilience measures through the Community Rating System (CRS). However, disparities in CRS ratings and the frequency of hazard map updates suggest gaps in preparedness, leaving residents in vulnerable regions underinsured and at greater risk of financial loss.

What Are CRS Ratings?

The Community Rating System (CRS) is a FEMA initiative under the NFIP that encourages communities to exceed minimum disaster preparedness standards. Communities earn a CRS class rating between 1 (highest) and 9 (lowest) based on their efforts in areas such as public information, hazard mapping, regulation, and damage reduction.

Higher CRS ratings result in greater flood insurance premium discounts for residents:

  • Class 1: 45% discount
  • Class 9: 5% discount

Communities with low CRS ratings often lack the resources or technical expertise to implement advanced hurricane resilience practices, reducing their residents' eligibility for meaningful insurance discounts.

Local Governance

FEMA provides funding and technical assistance to communities through programs like the NFIP, hazard mitigation grants, and disaster relief. However, the effectiveness of these funds depends on how well local governments apply for and manage the resources.

Funding Distribution: FEMA funding often requires local governments to provide matching funds or meet specific criteria, which can be challenging for resource-constrained areas.

Local Governance Variability: Local governments in the Southeast vary widely in their capacity to enforce zoning laws, maintain infrastructure, and participate in programs like the CRS. Smaller or under-resourced municipalities may struggle to apply for FEMA grants, update hazard maps, or adopt advanced hurricane resilience practices.

Hazard Maps Hazard maps, created by FEMA, identify areas at high risk for hurricanes and are used to set insurance rates and guide development decisions. The "Currently Effective Map Date" indicates the last time a community's hazard map was updated. Many communities in the Southeast rely on outdated maps that fail to reflect current risks due to climate change or urban development. Updating these maps is costly and requires local initiative and funding, which some communities lack.

So What?

The combination of low CRS ratings, outdated hazard maps, and inconsistent local governance leaves many Southeast communities ill-prepared for hurricanes. Residents face higher insurance premiums, reduced disaster resilience, and greater financial vulnerability after major storms.

Hurricane Helene

On September 24, 2024, a catastrophic Category 4 hurricane named Helene struck the state of Florida, leaving devastation in its path. Flooding, high winds, rainfall, a storm surge, and dangerous currents ravaged the land and communities of Florida, leaving hundreds dead or missing. The storm continued to impact other states, including Georgia, South Carolina, North Carolina, Tennessee, and Virginia. As the Southeast recovers from this major tropical storm, questions remain about what causes these powerful cyclones and what can be done to prevent them in the future. FEMA EMPG State Disparities

The FEMA Emergency Management Performance Grants (EMPG) provide funding to state, local, tribal, and territorial governments to enhance their emergency management systems and capabilities. The grants support activities such as planning, training, exercises, and the development of emergency operations centers. EMPG aims to improve preparedness, response, and recovery capabilities for all types of hazards, helping communities better manage disasters. CONCLUSION

Targeted interventions are crucial to addressing the disparities in disaster preparedness and resilience, particularly in southeastern states. Increasing federal support for regular flood map updates would provide communities with accurate, up-to-date data to better assess flood risks and implement effective floodplain management strategies. Enhancing technical assistance for local governments would help them interpret hazard maps, adopt best practices for flood mitigation, and increase participation in the National Flood Insurance Program (NFIP), ensuring financial preparedness for disasters. Additionally, promoting equitable disaster preparedness practices is essential to ensure that marginalized communities, who are often the most vulnerable, have access to resources, information, and affordable insurance. These interventions would empower underserved populations, helping them better prepare for and recover from hurricane and flood events, while ensuring that resilience efforts are inclusive and effective across all communities.
